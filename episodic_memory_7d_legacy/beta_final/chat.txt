This is a great problem — let me lay out the key approaches and tradeoffs, then show you a visual architecture.

The core challenge is that your fingerprint needs to capture similarity in a way that predicts outcome quality, not just raw feature distance. Here are the main strategies:

Approach 1: Hand-crafted feature vector

Extract a normalized vector from the initial conditions: start/end zone IDs (or their coordinates discretized into grid cells), estimated distance (bucketed or normalized), and obstacle density (e.g., low/medium/high = 0/0.5/1). Concatenate and compute cosine or Euclidean similarity. Simple, interpretable, fast — and with 120 missions you probably have enough density to make it work.

Approach 2: Semantic zone encoding

Rather than raw start/end coordinates, encode the semantic type of the zones (corridor, open hall, lab, doorway cluster, etc.) plus their connectivity. This captures that "lab-to-corridor" missions have more in common than two missions that happen to start 2m apart in different room types.

Approach 3: Learned embedding (recommended for your scale)

Train a small autoencoder or contrastive model on your 120 missions to learn a low-dimensional latent fingerprint. Missions that led to similar outcomes cluster together, not just missions with similar inputs. At 120 samples this is tight but feasible if you use heavy regularization or a pre-trained distance metric. The fingerprint then becomes a 4–8 dimensional vector.

Approach 4: Obstacle density profile

Instead of a single density scalar, encode a spatial profile — a mini-histogram of obstacle density along the expected path corridor. This discriminates between "dense at start" vs "dense midway" which is critical for control parameter selection.

Now for the specific fingerprint construction I'd recommend for your situation:

Recommended fingerprint (pragmatic for 120 missions)

Build a vector with these components, each independently normalized to [0,1]:

Zone pair hash — a categorical embedding of (start_zone, end_zone) pairs. With k zones you get k² possible pairs; since 120 missions won't cover all pairs, use a simple integer index and store it alongside the continuous features.
Normalized distance — divide by the max observed mission distance in your dataset.
Obstacle density at start — your existing scalar.
Path-corridor density profile — discretize the expected path into 3–4 segments and store mean density per segment. This adds 3–4 dimensions but dramatically improves discrimination.
Zone type flags — binary or ordinal encoding of the semantic type of start and end locations (narrow corridor, open space, doorway, elevator, etc.) if you have that metadata.
This gives you a ~8–10 dimensional vector. For retrieval, use weighted cosine similarity where you assign higher weight to zone pair and density profile, and lower weight to distance alone. Keep the weights tunable — you'll want to calibrate them as your episode count grows.

One important practical note: with only 120 missions, your kNN retrieval will sometimes return neighbors that are geometrically close in fingerprint space but led to very different outcomes due to noise or unobserved factors. Add an outcome quality filter — only retrieve episodes whose post-mission efficiency and safety scores are above some threshold. This turns it from pure similarity search into similarity-weighted quality retrieval, which is much more useful for seeding the perturbation loop.

The improvement loop math: the perturbation magnitude should shrink as episode density increases around the new query fingerprint. When the 3 retrieved neighbors are very close (cosine similarity > 0.95), use small noise (σ ≈ 2–5% of param range). When they're more distant (< 0.8), explore more aggressively (σ ≈ 10–15%). This gives you adaptive exploration — tighter exploitation in well-covered regions, broader search in sparse ones.

Good — clean and bounded. With 4 raw inputs (6 scalar values), here's how to think about the fingerprint design space:

The core tension: start/end coordinates are 2D positions in a space, not independent scalars. Treating them as 4 separate normalized numbers loses their geometric relationship. The relative geometry — direction, displacement, distance — often matters more than absolute positions.

Recommended fingerprint: 7-dimensional vector

Why these 7 components specifically:

f1, f2 — normalized positions: divide by 80 (the full range) so both axes map to [0,1]. These anchor the mission spatially — a robot starting near a wall behaves differently than one starting in the center.

f3 — heading angle: the direction from start to end, normalized to [-1,1]. This is arguably the most important single feature. Two missions with similar positions but opposite headings are almost completely dissimilar in terms of useful control parameters.

f4 — straight-line distance: normalize by 56.57m (the diagonal of your 80×80 space, the maximum possible straight-line distance). Use this rather than re-using estimated distance — it's a clean geometric fact independent of obstacles.

f5 — tortuosity: estimated_distance / straight_line_distance. A ratio ≥ 1 that tells you how "winding" the path must be. A tortuosity of 1.0 means open straight path; 2.0+ means heavy detour required. This is where obstacle complexity shows up as path shape, not just a scalar density.

f6, f7 — density and congestion: density alone is useful, but density × tortuosity as a single composite gives you a scalar that captures "how hard is this mission overall" — a natural retrieval discriminator.

On your 120-mission dataset:

With this 7-dim space, plot your missions in PCA or t-SNE before deploying. If you see natural clusters (e.g. short-range dense vs long-range open), your kNN will work well out of the box. If the cloud is uniform, consider adding a zone quadrant flag (which of 4 quadrants does start and end fall in) as a categorical override — it prevents returning a geometrically similar mission that happens to be on the opposite side of the building where obstacles are laid out differently.

One implementation tip: store the raw inputs alongside the fingerprint in every episode JSON. As your dataset grows past ~500 missions you may want to retrain the weighting or switch to a learned embedding — having the originals means you never need to re-run missions to rebuild the index.
